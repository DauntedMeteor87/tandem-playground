01
Philosophy

Architecture philosophy & the 5 ways you're most likely to shoot yourself in the foot

Your situation has three defining facts, and the architecture should bend to all three: (1) you are a solo, newer engineer learning fast, with Claude Code and other AI as your team; (2) the product is unusually media-heavy for a pre-seed app — video is a first-class feature, not a nice-to-have; and (3) your edge is product and community (per your own "Note to Self" — free, Gen-Z, novel experiences, local density), not infrastructure. That last point is the whole strategy: infrastructure is a cost center you want to make boring, cheap, and bought; the product is where your one pair of hands goes.

The philosophy in four rules

  • Buy the hard parts, build the product. Video transcoding, auth, search, email, push, payments — these are solved commodities sold by specialists. Building them yourself is how a solo founder spends a year and ships nothing. Your code should be the social graph, the feed, the plans, the messaging UX — the things that make Kindred Kindred.
  • Boring, typed, explicit tech. Claude Code is dramatically more reliable on technologies with massive training data and strong type systems (TypeScript, Postgres, React). Exotic or weakly-typed stacks force the model to guess, and a guessing AI writing most of your code is how technical debt compounds invisibly.
  • One deployable, with clean internal seams. A modular monolith (one app, strict module boundaries) gives you a serious-company structure without the operational nightmare of microservices. The seams are where you'll later cut out a service — if you ever actually need to.
  • Abstract what you might replace; couple to what's a standard. Put a thin interface in front of every vendor you could swap (video, auth, analytics). Couple freely to Postgres-the-engine and to your language — those are portable standards, not lock-in.
Jargon: monolith vs. modular monolith vs. microservices

A monolith is one program that does everything — simplest to build and deploy. Microservices split the app into many small programs that talk over the network — flexible at huge scale, but each one needs its own deploy, monitoring, database connection, and failure handling (operational tax a solo founder cannot pay). A modular monolith is the sweet spot: one program and one deploy, but internally divided into well-isolated modules (auth, feed, media…) that only talk through defined interfaces. You get clean boundaries now and the option to extract a module into its own service later, without paying the microservices tax today.

The 5 ways you're most likely to shoot yourself in the foot

Trap 1 — Building video infrastructure (or a CapCut clone) yourself

Video is your hardest problem and biggest bill (Section 2). The temptation to "just use FFmpeg and S3" will cost you months and produce something worse than a $50/mo managed service. The same applies to in-app editing: a multi-layer timeline editor is a multi-year, multi-engineer product on its own. Launch with a managed video provider and basic on-device editing. Do not build either from scratch.

Trap 2 — Premature microservices, Kubernetes, or multi-cloud

Every "scalable architecture" blog pushes you toward distributed systems. At 1k–100k users this is pure self-sabotage: you'll spend your time debugging service-to-service networking and YAML instead of building features. Kubernetes, service meshes, multi-region, and multi-cloud are answers to problems you do not have. A modular monolith on a PaaS will comfortably carry you past 100k users.

Trap 3 — Letting Claude Code quietly accumulate architectural debt

This is the subtle one, and the most dangerous given your setup. An AI will happily add a new dependency, invent a fifth way to call the database, or smear business logic across controllers — and it all looks like progress. Without guardrails, you wake up at month six with an unmaintainable codebase you didn't consciously design. The fix is process (Section 9): a strong CLAUDE.md, enforced module boundaries, tests as guardrails the agent must keep green, architecture decision records, and reviewing every diff. The AI writes the code; you own the architecture.

Trap 4 — Coupling tightly to one vendor's proprietary APIs

If your business logic calls Mux (or Supabase, or Algolia) directly from a hundred places, switching providers later — for cost, features, or because they 10×'d their price — becomes a terrifying rewrite. One thin interface per swappable vendor (Section 8) turns a rewrite into a one-file adapter swap. This is the cheapest insurance you'll ever buy.

Trap 5 — Treating trust & safety as a "later" problem

You host user-generated video, your target users are 18–24, and your product encourages them to meet strangers in person for outdoor adventures. That is a serious trust-and-safety and liability surface. A single CSAM upload, a predatory DM, or an unsafe meetup that you facilitated could be existential — legally and for the brand of trust your "Note to Self" is built on. Automated content moderation, reporting/blocking, and a safety posture are launch requirements, not v2 features (Section 11).

There is a sixth, quieter mistake worth naming: flying blind. If you don't instrument analytics from the first commit, you'll make product decisions on vibes and have no data to feed the AI features you want later. The opposite failure — over-building infra before you have product-market fit — is covered in Section 15. Both are the same disease: spending effort on the wrong layer for your stage.

Verdict

Adopt the "buy hard parts, build product, stay boring and typed, abstract the swappable" philosophy. Your three real enemies are: building infrastructure you should rent, letting an AI design your architecture by accident, and ignoring trust & safety. Everything else in this memo is downstream of getting those three right.

02
The hard part

The video problem — your hardest engineering challenge and biggest bill

Treat this as the most important technical decision in the memo. Video has four distinct stages — upload → transcoding → storage → adaptive delivery — and for a user-generated-content (UGC) app, storage and egress (delivery bandwidth) are the costs that quietly kill you, not the fancy encoding. Get this wrong and you either burn months building a pipeline or get a five-figure surprise bill.

Jargon: the video pipeline, in plain terms

Transcoding = converting one uploaded file into many versions (different resolutions/bitrates). Adaptive bitrate streaming (ABR / HLS) = the player automatically picks the right version for the viewer's network, switching on the fly so video doesn't buffer. Egress = the bandwidth cost of sending video out to viewers — on raw cloud (AWS) this is the single largest and most dangerous line item. Just-in-time encoding = only creating a version when someone actually watches, which saves storage (Mux pioneered this).

Build vs. buy: this is not a close call

buy A managed video provider gives you upload, transcoding, ABR, a global CDN, a player, and analytics for cents per minute. Building the equivalent yourself means operating FFmpeg transcoding workers, an HLS packager, storage lifecycle rules, a multi-CDN delivery layer, and a player — a dedicated team's job. As a solo founder, building video infrastructure is the single fastest way to fail. You buy this until video is a top-three cost line and you have engineers to run a pipeline. Because you'll put it behind a MediaService interface (Section 8), switching providers later is a contained change, not a rewrite.

On-device vs. cloud editing, and the road to "CapCut-level"

Editing splits into two questions: where does it run, and how good is it?

  • Launch = on-device basic editing. Trim, crop, text, filters, and music can all run on the phone using the platform's native frameworks (iOS AVFoundation, Android Media3/MediaCodec), exposed to your React Native app via a native module. On-device is free (no server cost), private, and instant. This matches Instagram's basic editor and is the right v1.
  • CapCut-level is a multi-year product, not a feature. A multi-layer timeline, keyframed effects, and high-quality export is what CapCut's large team builds full-time. Do not attempt it in v1. The realistic path: basic on-device → license a commercial video-editing SDK (e.g. Banuba, BytePlus) for richer effects → cloud rendering for heavy exports at scale. Crucially, your framework choice (React Native) does not block this — the editing layer is native work regardless of whether you chose React Native, Flutter, or fully native.
  • Cloud editing (server-side rendering of effects/exports) becomes worth it only when on-device can't deliver the quality you want at export — a Phase 3 concern.
The music feature is a licensing problem, not a storage problem

Storing audio is trivially cheap (objects in R2/S3, metadata in Postgres) — even "Spotify-quality" local-artist uploads cost pennies. The genuinely hard part of a music library is rights and sync licensing: what can legally be played over a video, who owns it, and how creators license their uploads to you and to other users. Architect the easy part now (an audio_tracks table + object storage + a MusicService interface) and treat licensing as the legal/ops workstream it actually is. Kindred-specific + local-artist-upload is a smart wedge precisely because it sidesteps the major-label licensing minefield.

Where it runs

Hosting & cloud — through the scale-ready lens

The mistake here is choosing AWS on day one "because that's what real companies use" and then drowning in IAM policies and YAML as a solo founder. The opposite mistake is choosing a toy that you outgrow at 50k users. The scale-ready answer is a Platform-as-a-Service (PaaS) that removes server management now, on a foundation (containers + Postgres + standard runtimes) that ports cleanly to AWS later when you actually need it.

Jargon: PaaS vs. IaaS, and "lock-in"

IaaS (AWS, GCP, Azure) rents you raw building blocks — servers, networks, disks — and you assemble and operate everything. Maximum power, maximum DevOps. PaaS (Render, Railway, Fly.io, Vercel) takes your code and runs it for you — you push, it deploys. Less control, far less operational burden. Lock-in risk = how painful it is to leave. Running a standard Docker container + Postgres on a PaaS is low lock-in (it moves to AWS easily); building on a cloud's proprietary glue (Lambda + DynamoDB + 12 AWS services wired together) is high lock-in.

04
The source of truth

Database strategy — PostgreSQL, and when to add everything else

You suspected PostgreSQL, and you're right. Postgres is the correct default and will be the spine of Kindred from 1k to 500k users and well beyond. The art is knowing what to add alongside it, and — more importantly — what not to add yet.

Why Postgres is right for Kindred specifically

  • Your data is deeply relational. Users follow users, posts belong to users, plans contain items, messages belong to conversations. This is a textbook relational graph, and Postgres enforces its integrity with foreign keys and transactions.
  • It's also flexible where you need it. JSONB columns store schema-less metadata (video render details, flexible event props) without a separate document database. You get relational rigor and document flexibility in one engine.
  • It's pre-armed for your roadmap. PostGIS is ready the day you add GPS (so you never "architect yourself out of it"), pgvector is ready for AI embeddings and recommendations, and built-in full-text search covers your search needs early — all without new infrastructure.
  • Claude Code knows it cold, and it's the most battle-tested open-source database on earth. Boring, in the best way.
Jargon: SQL vs. NoSQL and the database families

SQL / relational (Postgres, MySQL): data in tables with enforced relationships; best for structured, interconnected data — your default. NoSQL is an umbrella for non-relational stores: document (MongoDB — flexible JSON blobs), key-value (Redis — ultra-fast lookups by key), columnar (ClickHouse, BigQuery — analytics over billions of rows), time-series (for metrics/GPS streams). OLTP = fast small reads/writes for the live app (Postgres). OLAP = heavy analytical queries over huge history (a warehouse). You start with OLTP Postgres and add an OLAP warehouse only when analytics outgrow it.

Jargon: the operational concepts you'll actually touch

Index = a lookup structure that makes queries fast (without one, the DB scans every row). Join = combining related tables in a query. Transaction = a group of changes that all succeed or all fail together (so you never half-create a post). Replication / read replica = a copy of the DB that serves reads, spreading load. Partitioning = splitting one big table into chunks (e.g., messages by month) for performance. Sharding = splitting data across multiple separate databases — powerful but complex; avoid until forced. Backups / PITR (point-in-time recovery) = the ability to restore to any second before a disaster — non-negotiable for production.

Jargon: how the feed is generated

Two strategies. Fan-out-on-read (pull): when a user opens the app, query for recent posts from everyone they follow. Simple, always fresh, but slow for users following thousands. Fan-out-on-write (push): when someone posts, pre-insert it into each follower's precomputed feed. Fast reads, but expensive writes for popular accounts. Start with pull + caching (it's plenty for your scale and density-focused model); add precomputed feeds and a cache layer only when read latency demands it. This is exactly where Redis earns its place.

One language to rule them

Backend language & framework — TypeScript everywhere, NestJS at the core

This is the decision that most shapes how productive Claude Code will be for you, so weight that heavily. The recommendation is TypeScript on Node.js across your entire stack, with a NestJS modular monolith as the backend. The reason is not that TypeScript is the "best" language in the abstract — it's that one language shared across mobile, web, and API gives a solo founder leverage that no polyglot stack can match.

The decisive argument: shared types

When your API, your React Native app, and your Next.js web app are all TypeScript, a single packages/types defines your data shapes once. Change the Post type and every consumer — mobile, web, server — fails to compile until it's fixed. That compiler is a safety net specifically tuned for the way you'll work: large AI-generated changes, reviewed quickly. In a polyglot stack (Python API + TypeScript clients) that net disappears at the most dangerous boundary.

Swap providers without rewrites

Vendor abstraction — interfaces at the boundary, coupling at the core

You'll start on managed services to move fast (Mux, Supabase Auth, PostHog), but you don't want those choices welded into your code so that swapping one means a rewrite. The pattern is simple and powerful: define a thin interface for each external capability, and let the rest of your app depend only on that interface — never on the vendor's SDK directly. When you outgrow a provider, you write one new implementation of the interface; nothing else changes.

Jargon: the service-interface (port/adapter) pattern

You define a port — an interface describing what you need ("store a video and give me a playback ID"), not how. Each vendor gets an adapter — a concrete class implementing that interface using their SDK. Your app talks only to the port. This is the practical core of "design for migration, not premature duplication": you abstract the seam, but you don't build two providers up front — you build one adapter now and add another only when you actually migrate.

What this looks like in TypeScript

// The port: your app depends on THIS, not on Mux.
interface MediaService {
  createUploadUrl(opts: UploadOpts): Promise<{ uploadUrl: string; assetId: string }>;
  getPlayback(assetId: string): Promise<{ playbackId: string; status: AssetStatus }>;
  deleteAsset(assetId: string): Promise<void>;
}

// Adapter #1 — today. All Mux-specific code is quarantined in here.
class MuxMediaService implements MediaService { /* ...Mux SDK calls... */ }

// Adapter #2 — only written if/when you migrate. Nothing else changes.
class CloudflareStreamMediaService implements MediaService { /* ... */ }

// Your feature code stays vendor-agnostic forever:
class PostsService {
  constructor(private media: MediaService) {}   // injected — doesn't know or care which vendor
}

What to abstract vs. what to couple to

Over-abstraction is its own trap — wrapping everything creates needless indirection that slows you and confuses Claude Code. The rule: abstract behind an interface where lock-in is high and switching is plausible; couple freely where the dependency is a stable, portable foundation.

Verdict

Don't optimize infrastructure cost early; optimize your time. Do architect the video seam early, because that's the bill that eventually decides your margins. And always weigh the invisible engineering-complexity cost — for a solo founder it's often larger than the dollar figure.

The two ways founders hurt themselves

Mistakes to avoid — overkill on one side, genuine risk on the other

Technical-founder failure splits cleanly into two buckets, and they pull in opposite directions. One is overkill: building for a scale you don't have, which burns the time and money that actually kill early startups. The other is genuine risk: skipping the few things that are cheap now and catastrophic later. The skill is telling them apart — being lean on the first list and uncompromising on the second.

Overkill for your stage — resist these

The single most common technical-founder mistake

Above every specific item sits one meta-mistake: choosing infrastructure for the scale you dream of instead of the scale you have. Founders read about how TikTok or Discord operate and build a fraction of that machine before they have a thousand engaged users — and the complexity buries them before they ever find product-market fit. The correct mental model is "build for your next 10x, not for 500x." Solve 1k→10k now; design clean seams so 10k→100k is an evolution, not a rewrite; and trust that you'll have the users, money, and possibly a team to handle 100k→500k when you actually get there.

Verdict

Be ruthlessly lean on the overkill list — no Kubernetes, no microservices, no sharding, no warehouse, no SOC 2, no DIY auth/video/search until something real forces it — and absolutely uncompromising on the risk list: backups, moderation, secrets hygiene, error tracking, loose coupling, tests, and analytics, all from day one. The meta-rule beneath both: build for your next 10x, never someone else's 500x. That one sentence prevents the majority of technical-founder failures.
