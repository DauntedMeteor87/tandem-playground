Why We Open Apps: The Questions and Moods Behind Every Tap

An investigation into what 18-to-24-year-old iPhone users are actually looking for when they unlock their phones — the questions they're trying to answer, the mood states that drive the tap, which apps get opened first, and how it all collapses into three basic modes: browsing, messaging, and planning.

00 — Method & Data Rules

How to Read This Brief (And What We Refused to Do)

Every number in this document traces back to a named study with a stated sample size, linked in the references. Nothing is estimated, simulated, or invented. Where the exact data you'd want — iPhone users, ages 18–24, United States — does not exist publicly, this brief says so in plain text rather than papering over the gap.

One thing to know up front: Apple has never released its Screen Time data. There is no public Apple archive of which apps people open first or how often. Apple publishes exactly one thing — App Store download charts[5] — and downloads are not opens. Apple also restricts what researchers can log on iPhones; the largest recent youth phone-tracking study in the U.S. had to run on Android phones only, because, in the researchers' words, Apple's tools do not share the names of the specific apps young people use.[21] So the honest picture of iPhone behavior comes from a small number of studies where people photographed or uploaded their own Screen Time summaries — and from one heroic study that strapped cameras to people's glasses.

The rules this brief follows:
1. Every statistic carries a citation and every study's sample size (n) is stated in the text or the roster below.
2. Samples are labeled: iPhone · 18–24 means the number matches the target population exactly; nearby sample means close but not exact (the mismatch is spelled out); excluded means the study exists but was not counted because it misses the spec (wrong platform or age) — it appears only in clearly marked context boxes.
3. The box plots in Section 03 were computed by us from the raw, publicly posted participant-level dataset of Ellis et al.[2] — real people, real Screen Time numbers, filtered to ages 18–24. Our re-analysis reproduced the paper's published averages exactly before filtering, which confirms we used the same data the authors did.
4. No number in any chart was interpolated, modeled, or "filled in."

Why trust logged data over surveys? Because people are terrible at estimating their own phone use: in a meta-analysis covering tens of thousands of participants, fewer than 10% of people's self-reported screen-time numbers landed within 5% of what their device actually logged.[17] Wherever possible, this brief uses what phones recorded, not what people guessed.

01 — The Questions

Every App Open Is a Question Being Asked

Nobody opens an app for no reason. Underneath nearly every tap there is a question the person wants answered, even if they'd never say it out loud. Communication researchers have studied this for decades under the name uses and gratifications — the idea that people actively pick media to satisfy specific needs, rather than passively consuming whatever is in front of them.[7] Translated out of academic language, the needs are questions:

The same question can be asked on three clocks

Noah's intuition that Instagram is "midterm," iMessage "short term," and LinkedIn "long term" holds up as a useful second way to slice the data: the same question — what are my people up to? — gets routed to different apps depending on the time horizon of the answer. A text answers what's happening in the next hour. An Instagram story answers what happened this week (the Portugal photos). LinkedIn answers what happened to their life. The academic literature doesn't use the short/mid/long framing explicitly, but it's consistent with what each platform's own users report: Snapchat is used with a small circle of strong ties for immediate, playful contact (n = 209 first-year students college age),[9] while Instagram's dominant motive is keeping tabs on a wider circle over time.[8]

A third way to categorize — the one this brief builds to in Section 04 — ignores both the question's content and its clock, and sorts by what the person is doing: consuming (browsing), talking (messaging), or deciding (planning). Keep all three lenses in mind; they'll be tested against the behavioral data below.

02 — The Moods

Mood States: The Emotional Triggers Behind the Tap

Questions are the surface. Underneath, what actually launches a hand toward a phone is usually a feeling — and psychology has mapped several of these feelings onto phone behavior with real measurements. The umbrella theory is called mood management: people instinctively pick media that they expect will repair or maintain their emotional state.[16] Here is what the evidence says about each mood state, in roughly the order they show up during a normal day.

Boredom — "I need stimulation, now"

The cleanest test to date pinged 83 PhD candidates every hour at work while software silently logged their phones (n = 83 adults · at work). The more bored or tired people said they were, the more likely they were to open the phone in the next stretch — but here's the twist: they reported feeling more bored and tired after using it, not less.[13] The phone is reached for as a boredom cure and fails as one. Boredom opens the app; the app quietly re-supplies the boredom.

Noah's entertainment ladder — Netflix for long-form stimulation, YouTube midform, TikTok/Instagram short-form — matches how the same students' iPhones logged their time: TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat and YouTube alone made up 60% of "most-used app" records, with Netflix and Spotify further down the same list.[1] The shorter the content loop, the higher the app sits in the boredom rankings. (Full ranked list in Section 03.)

Stress and nervousness — the phone as a pacifier

A Journal of Consumer Research paper (one large field study plus three lab experiments adults) makes the case bluntly: smartphones function like adult pacifiers. Under induced stress, people preferentially reach for their own phone over other devices, and using it produces measurably more stress relief than doing the identical task on a laptop or on someone else's phone. The comfort comes from the phone being portable, personal, private, and physically in your hand.[14] The video-ethnography study saw the same thing in the wild: people checked their phones during stressful work "to give their minds a break."[3]

One honest complication: in the 12-week iPhone study of undergraduates iPhone · ~18–24, weeks of higher-than-usual stress did not produce measurably heavier phone use — usage was too habitual and stable to budge (a typical student's daily pickup count varied by about 17 week to week, with a spread of only ~32 pickups between their heaviest and lightest weeks of the whole semester).[1] Read together with the pacifier research: stress changes why and when you grab the phone more than it changes how much.

FoMO — "everyone is having fun without me"

Fear of missing out is the most-studied phone emotion. In the original scale-development work (n = 2,079 UK adults, with follow-up samples of young adults adults · UK), FoMO ran highest in younger people, was fueled by unmet needs for connection, and was the engine linking low mood and low life-satisfaction to heavier social media use. Young adults high in FoMO also paid more attention to incoming emails and texts.[10] Among U.S. undergraduates specifically (n = 296 college age), FoMO was the single strongest emotional correlate of both problematic and perfectly ordinary smartphone use — stronger than negative mood or fear of being judged.[11] FoMO is the mood state that turns a phone from a tool into a slot machine: the question it whispers is "did I miss something?", and the answer is always one unlock away.

Excitement and anticipation — "I can't wait, who else is hyped?"

Excitement is the least-measured mood state in the literature (few researchers ping people right before a soccer game). The closest hard evidence is indirect: the checking-habit research shows phone checks cluster around dynamic content that changes quickly — the informational reward of something new having arrived[15] — and the FoMO work shows anticipation of shared experiences drives attention to messaging channels.[10] Behaviorally, hype lives in group chats: messaging apps are where 18–24s' phones open first (Section 03), and Snapchat — the app most often opened on unlock in the student data[1] — is precisely the app students describe using for playful, immediate exchange with close friends.[9] A group chat before an event is a hype amplifier: the question "is everyone as excited as me?" gets answered in real time, meme by meme. No study we found measures pre-event group-chat behavior directly in this age band — that's a genuine gap, and a cheap one for Tandem to research itself.

Loneliness / low mood — the complicated one

Intuition says sad people scroll more. The logged data pushes back: in the one-week study that measured actual use frequency among college students (n = 68, ages 18–25 18–25 · logged), lower depression predicted increasing use across the week — and people who habitually bottle up emotions ("expressive suppression") started the week using their phones more.[12] And in the 12-week iPhone study, heavier-than-usual weeks didn't dent end-of-week mood; the only reliable pattern was between people: students who generally lived on their phones more reported being slightly more irritated and stressed on average.[1] The takeaway for app-builders: phone use tracks emotional habits more than emotional moments.

No mood at all — the habit loop

The biggest driver of opens is embarrassingly unglamorous: nothing. The founding study of "checking habits" (three datasets of usage logs adults · 2011 era) defined the signature pattern — brief, repetitive inspections of dynamic content, reinforced by quick informational rewards, experienced by users as annoyance rather than addiction.[15] A decade later, the camera-glasses study watched it happen live: people touched their phones roughly every five minutes, and when shown the footage were shocked — "I don't even remember picking it up."[3] The cognition driving most opens isn't a question or a feeling. It's a motor program: hand moves, thumb unlocks, apps get swept in a fixed personal order. Any app hoping to be opened on purpose is competing against ten opens that happen on autopilot.

03 — The Behavior

What Actually Gets Opened (iPhone, Ages 18–24)

This is the section where most reports would quietly switch to made-up numbers. Instead, here is precisely what exists. Two datasets meet the iPhone + college-age spec: the 12-week Canadian undergraduate study (n = 187, mean age 20.1, iPhone-only, weekly Screen Time uploads iPhone · ~18–24)[1] and the UK Screen Time dataset whose raw participant-level file is public, letting us cut it to exactly ages 18–24 (n = 62 of 238 iPhone · 18–24).[2]

3.1 — The first app opened after pickup

Apple's Screen Time counts a "pickup" every time you lift the phone from idle and interact, and records which app you went to first. Across 860 usable weekly reports from the 187 students, three apps dominated the first-tap-after-unlock records:[1]

Fig. 3 — First app opened after unlocking, share of records iPhone · ~18–24
Share of records in which each app was the most- or second-most-accessed app immediately after unlocking. Bradley & Howard (2023), n = 187 iPhone-using undergraduates, 12 weekly device-logged reports.[1] Remaining ~35% spread across all other apps.

Read that again: two of the top three first-opens are messaging apps (Snapchat functions primarily as messaging for this cohort — students describe it as an easier, funnier texting channel for close friends[9]). The first instinct on unlocking is overwhelmingly who said something to me / who can I say something to, and only then the feed.

The London video study — the only research that literally watched what app people went to (n = 37, ages 21–29 21–29 · mixed devices) — found the same shape with UK app preferences: WhatsApp was the destination of 22% of pickups, Instagram 16%, Facebook 13%, while phone calls, SMS and maps each accounted for only about 1%.[3] Different country, different messenger, same instinct: message first, browse second.

Why no per-app box plots? A box plot needs the full spread of individual values. 3.5 — Notifications vs. the home screen: what actually triggers the open

The popular story is that notifications cause phone use. The only study that filmed real life found the opposite: 89% of phone interactions were started by the person, unprompted; only 11% were responses to a notification (1,130 interactions, n = 37, ages 21–29 21–29 · mixed devices).[3]

Fig. 10 — Who starts the interaction?
1,130 filmed smartphone interactions. Heitmayer & Lahlou (2021), n = 37, ages 21–29.[3]

Set that beside the notification volume from the 18–24 iPhone data above: the median young user receives ~118 notifications a day but initiates the overwhelming majority of opens themselves. In the full 238-person dataset, pickups and notifications arrive at an almost perfectly matched rate — 1 pickup per 1.05 notifications[2] — yet the filmed evidence shows the notification usually isn't the trigger; it's ambient. People don't answer their phones. They visit them, on an internal clock, roughly every five minutes,[3] driven by the checking habit described in Section 02.[15]

Which apps ride each channel? For this age band on iPhone, no public dataset splits opens into notification-led vs. home-screen-led per app — that's a real gap, stated plainly. The best available evidence is directional: messaging apps dominate both the first-open records (Fig. 3) and the filmed destinations (Fig. 4) — and messages are the notification type most likely to be about a specific person wanting you — while feed apps (TikTok, Instagram-as-feed, YouTube) are the classic self-initiated, "visit" apps of the checking habit.[3][15] Younger age also predicts more of everything: in the raw data, age correlates negatively with both pickups (r = −.32) and notifications (r = −.35).[2]

Is the browse / message / plan grouping actually accurate?

Noah's proposed trichotomy was tested against every dataset in this brief. Verdict: it holds, with two honest amendments.

What fits beautifully. The two biggest behavioral facts in the data are exactly the first two buckets. Messaging owns the opens — half of all first-app-after-unlock records are Snapchat plus Messages,[1] and the filmed studies route the plurality of pickups to a messenger.[3] Browsing owns the hours — four feed/video apps swallow 60% of most-used-app records.[1] The mood evidence divides the same way: FoMO and social hunger drive the message bucket,[10][11] while boredom and mood-repair drive the browse bucket.[13][16]

Amendment 1 — call the third bucket "plan/act," and admit it's the smallest. Utility opens (maps, calendar, weather, banking, camera, tickets) are real but each is a sliver — filmed at roughly 1% per app.[3] Grouped together as "taking action," they earn their column — and notice that the #1 downloaded iPhone app of 2025 was ChatGPT,[5] an instrumental, question-answering tool: the act bucket is where new demand is showing up, even while incumbent apps under-serve it. The interesting product fact is the asymmetry: 80% of people cite information-seeking as a reason they open apps,[7] yet planning apps barely register in open counts. The intent exists; no app owns it. That gap is Tandem's opening.

Amendment 2 — the buckets are modes, not apps. Instagram is the clearest case: it appears in the first-open (message-ish) records and in the most-hours (browse) records.[1] The same app gets opened in DM-mode when the mood is social and in feed-mode when the mood is bored. Snapchat's own users describe it as a messaging tool that happens to have content attached.[9] So the grouping is accurate as long as it classifies the state the user is in, not the icon they tap. Big platforms succeed precisely by bundling all three modes behind one icon and letting the feed capture time that arrived as a message-check — the mechanism behind the "I don't even remember picking it up" habit loop.[3][15]

05 — Conclusion

Three Moods, Three Modes, One Phone

Strip away the icons and the average 18-to-24-year-old's ~786 weekly phone opens[2] are doing three jobs. Browse: I'm bored or avoiding something — fill the moment (largest share of hours, and the mood research says the relief doesn't last[1][13]). Message: I might be missing something, I want my people — check the thread (largest share of opens; FoMO is its engine[1][10][11]). Plan: I'm ready to do something real — where, when, who's in (the most-stated intent with the least-served footprint[3][7]).

And the trigger for nearly nine in ten of those opens is internal — mood and habit, not a buzz from outside.[3] Whoever understands the mood, owns the open.

Side note — integration into Tandem (the three-state app): this research is the rationale for Tandem's proposed three-state navigation bar, where each state matches one of the three verified modes rather than one feature: top — "I'm just looking" (browse: trips, spots, inspiration), middle — messaging (the group chat, because messaging is the proven front door of every unlock[1][3]), bottom — "I'm excited, let's plan" (dates, RSVPs, logistics — the high-intent, unowned mode[3][7]). The design bet, supported by Section 04: users don't switch apps, they switch moods — so let one app switch with them, and route the browse-mode user toward the plan-mode exit that no incumbent offers.
