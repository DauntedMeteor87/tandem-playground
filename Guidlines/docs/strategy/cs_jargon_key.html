A plain-English guide to the hard words in the Ozola architecture memo. Use this to understand what the words mean, why they matter, and how to sound educated without pretending to be an expert.

How to use this key

1. Learn the idea, not the buzzword

Most CS jargon is just a short label for a practical problem: speed, cost, safety, organization, or future flexibility.

2. Ask trade-off questions

Good engineers do not just ask “can we build it?” They ask “what do we gain, what do we risk, and what do we postpone?”

3. Repeat this sentence

“I want to build for the next 10x of scale, not copy a giant company’s final architecture.”

Core mental model: buy the hard commodity parts, build the product-specific parts, keep the code boring and typed, and put clean seams around vendors you may replace later.

Tip: search “video”, “database”, “security”, “AI”, “scale”, “Claude”, or “vendor”.

Big-picture architecture

Architecturejargon

Means
The big-picture plan for how all the parts of an app fit together.

Think of it like
Like a blueprint for a house before you start building walls.

Say this

I’m trying to keep the architecture simple now, but leave room to scale later.

Backendjargon

Means
The hidden server-side part of an app that stores data, checks permissions, runs business logic, and talks to other services.

Think of it like
The kitchen in a restaurant. Users see the dining room, but the food is made in the back.

Say this

For Ozola, the backend handles posts, feeds, users, messaging, video metadata, and trip plans.

Frontendjargon

Means
The part users directly see and touch: mobile screens, buttons, forms, feeds, and web pages.

Think of it like
The dashboard and steering wheel of a car.

Say this

The frontend should stay fast and simple while the backend does the heavy lifting.

Full-stackjargon

Means
Working across both frontend and backend.

Think of it like
Someone who can build both the restaurant dining room and the kitchen systems.

Say this

I’m trying to understand the full stack so I can talk through the whole product.

Stackjargon

Means
The set of technologies used to build the app.

Think of it like
A toolbelt: language, database, hosting, analytics, video provider, etc.

Say this

The recommended stack is TypeScript, React Native, Next.js, NestJS, Postgres, Supabase, Render, Vercel, Cloudflare, and Mux.

Scalejargon

Means
The ability for the app to keep working as more users, data, and traffic arrive.

Think of it like
A coffee shop handling 10 customers versus 10,000 customers per day.

Say this

I want the system to be scale-ready, not overbuilt.

Scale-ready, not scale-overbuiltjargon

Means
Build in a way that can grow later, without paying for huge-company complexity today.

Think of it like
Buying a house with space to add rooms later, not building a hotel before you have guests.

Say this

The goal is to build for the next 10x, not for 500x on day one.

Migrationjargon

Means
Moving from one tool, vendor, database host, or architecture to another.

Think of it like
Moving apartments without changing your entire life.

Say this

We should design for migration so switching video providers later is not a rewrite.

Monolithjargon

Means
One backend program that contains all the app’s main features.

Think of it like
One big restaurant kitchen that makes every dish.

Say this

A monolith is simplest to build and deploy early.

Modular monolithjargon

Means
One backend program, but split internally into clean modules like auth, feed, media, messaging, and plans.

Think of it like
One building with clearly labeled rooms instead of a messy warehouse.

Say this

A modular monolith gives us clean boundaries without microservices complexity.

Microservicesjargon

Means
Many small backend services that each do one job and communicate over the network.

Think of it like
A restaurant where every dish station is in a separate building and they send ingredients by courier.

Say this

Microservices are powerful at huge scale but expensive to operate too early.

Operational taxjargon

Means
The extra time, complexity, and mental overhead required to run a system.

Think of it like
Owning five cars means five insurance bills, five maintenance schedules, and five chances something breaks.

Say this

Microservices add operational tax a solo founder probably cannot pay yet.

Distributed systemsjargon

Means
Systems made of multiple computers/services that must coordinate over a network.

Think of it like
A group project where everyone is in different time zones and messages can get lost.

Say this

Distributed systems are harder because networks fail, services go down, and debugging gets complicated.

Service extractionjargon

Means
Taking one module from a monolith and turning it into its own separate service when it truly needs independent scaling.

Think of it like
Turning the busiest food station into its own dedicated kitchen after demand proves it needs one.

Say this

We should extract chat or video workers only when metrics show a bottleneck.

Hot pathjargon

Means
The part of the system used constantly or requiring high performance.

Think of it like
The main checkout lane at a crowded grocery store.

Say this

Video playback and messaging may become hot paths.

Bottleneckjargon

Means
The slowest or most limiting part of a system.

Think of it like
A traffic jam caused by one narrow bridge.

Say this

We should improve bottlenecks based on measurements, not guesses.

Build vs. buyjargon

Means
Choosing whether to create something yourself or use a paid managed service.

Think of it like
Cooking every ingredient from scratch versus buying bread from a bakery.

Say this

Buy video, auth, search, email, and moderation early; build your product.

Infra / Infrastructurejargon

Means
The technical foundation that runs the app: hosting, databases, networks, storage, queues, monitoring.

Think of it like
Roads, power, and plumbing for software.

Say this

Infrastructure is a cost center; Ozola’s product is the differentiator.

Premature scalingjargon

Means
Adding complex scale infrastructure before current scale requires it.

Think of it like
Building an airport for a lemonade stand.

Say this

No Kubernetes, sharding, or microservices at launch.

Premature optimizationjargon

Means
Improving for a future problem before it exists, often wasting time.

Think of it like
Buying racing tires before you own a car.

Say this

Do not optimize infrastructure cost at 1k users when product speed matters more.

Code, apps, and APIs

TypeScriptjargon

Means
JavaScript plus a type system, meaning it can catch many mistakes before the app runs.

Think of it like
Writing labels on every box before moving so you know what belongs where.

Say this

TypeScript helps Claude Code and humans avoid mismatched data shapes.

Type systemjargon

Means
Rules that define what kind of data each variable or function expects.

Think of it like
A form that refuses a phone number in the email field.

Say this

Strong types are guardrails for AI-generated code.

Shared typesjargon

Means
Using the same data definitions across mobile, web, and backend.

Think of it like
Everyone reading from the same dictionary.

Say this

If Post changes, mobile and backend both fail to compile until they agree.

Compilejargon

Means
Convert/check code before it runs. In TypeScript, compilation also catches type errors.

Think of it like
Proofreading a recipe before cooking.

Say this

A compile error is often better than a runtime bug users see.

Runtimejargon

Means
When the code is actually running.

Think of it like
Cooking the recipe, not proofreading it.

Say this

Some errors only show up at runtime, which is why tests and monitoring matter.

Node.jsjargon

Means
A way to run JavaScript/TypeScript on servers instead of only in browsers.

Think of it like
Using the same language in the kitchen and the dining room.

Say this

Node is good for API work that mostly waits on databases and network calls.

NestJSjargon

Means
An opinionated TypeScript backend framework that organizes code into modules, controllers, and services.

Think of it like
A labeled filing cabinet for backend code.

Say this

NestJS gives Claude Code rails so it does not put logic everywhere.

Frameworkjargon

Means
A structured toolkit that tells you where code should go and gives common features out of the box.

Think of it like
A meal kit with instructions and ingredients instead of random groceries.

Say this

NestJS is the backend framework; Next.js is the web framework.

Dependency injectionjargon

Means
A pattern where code receives the tools it needs from the outside instead of creating them directly.

Think of it like
A chef is handed the right pan instead of building a pan every time.

Say this

Dependency injection makes it easier to swap MediaService implementations.

Controllerjargon

Means
Backend code that receives incoming API requests and sends responses.

Think of it like
The front desk that takes a request and routes it to the right department.

Say this

Controllers should stay thin and hand work to services.

Servicejargon

Means
Backend code that contains business logic, like creating a post or processing a follow.

Think of it like
The department that actually does the work after the front desk receives a request.

Say this

PostsService should know post logic but not vendor-specific video details.

Repositoryjargon

Means
Code responsible for reading and writing database records.

Think of it like
A librarian who knows exactly where to store and retrieve books.

Say this

Repositories help keep database access consistent.

Business logicjargon

Means
The app-specific rules that define how the product works.

Think of it like
The rules of a game, not the board itself.

Say this

Business logic should not be scattered across controllers.

React Nativejargon

Means
A framework for building iPhone and Android apps using React and TypeScript.

Think of it like
One design/build process that can produce apps for two phone platforms.

Say this

React Native lets a solo founder move faster than maintaining Swift and Kotlin separately.

Expojargon

Means
A toolkit around React Native that makes building, testing, and shipping mobile apps easier.

Think of it like
Training wheels plus a toolbox for React Native.

Say this

Expo reduces native build pain early.

Native modulejargon

Means
A piece of iOS/Android-specific code exposed to React Native for performance-critical features.

Think of it like
Calling in a specialist for one difficult part of the job.

Say this

Camera and editing features may need native modules.

Swiftjargon

Means
Apple’s main language for iOS apps.

Think of it like
The native language of iPhone development.

Say this

You might use Swift only for iOS video hot paths.

Kotlinjargon

Means
A main language for Android apps.

Think of it like
The native language of Android development.

Say this

You might use Kotlin only for Android video hot paths.

Next.jsjargon

Means
A React framework for building web apps, especially fast pages and shareable public pages.

Think of it like
A website-building framework with routing, rendering, and deployment patterns built in.

Say this

Next.js is a good fit for the companion web app.

APIjargon

Means
A structured way for software systems to talk to each other.

Think of it like
A restaurant menu for software: here are the requests you can make and what you get back.

Say this

The mobile app talks to the backend through an API.

RESTjargon

Means
A common API style where clients use URLs and HTTP methods like GET, POST, PATCH, and DELETE.

Think of it like
A predictable menu with standard action buttons.

Say this

REST is stable for mobile because old app versions may stay installed for months.

OpenAPIjargon

Means
A formal document that describes a REST API so tools can generate clients and documentation.

Think of it like
A precise instruction manual for your API.

Say this

OpenAPI helps generate a typed client for mobile and web.

tRPCjargon

Means
A TypeScript-first API approach where frontend and backend share types very tightly.

Think of it like
Two teammates using the same private shorthand.

Say this

tRPC is great in a monorepo but can couple mobile too tightly to backend changes.

Versioned APIjargon

Means
An API designed so older app versions keep working after the backend evolves.

Think of it like
Keeping old charger ports supported while new phones roll out.

Say this

Mobile apps need versioned APIs because users do not update instantly.

SDKjargon

Means
Software development kit: vendor-provided code for using their service.

Think of it like
A prebuilt remote control for a vendor’s product.

Say this

Do not call vendor SDKs from everywhere; wrap them in adapters.

API clientjargon

Means
Code used by the frontend to call the backend API.

Think of it like
A waiter who knows exactly how to place orders with the kitchen.

Say this

Generate a typed API client from OpenAPI.

Databases and data

PostgreSQL / Postgresjargon

Means
A powerful relational database used to store structured app data.

Think of it like
A very organized spreadsheet system that can enforce rules and relationships.

Say this

Postgres should be Ozola’s source of truth.

Databasejargon

Means
The place where app data is stored and queried.

Think of it like
The app’s long-term memory.

Say this

Users, posts, follows, messages, plans, and interactions live in the database.

Relational databasejargon

Means
A database built around tables that connect to each other.

Think of it like
Multiple spreadsheets linked by IDs.

Say this

Social apps have many relationships, so relational databases fit well.

Tablejargon

Means
A structured set of rows in a database, like users or posts.

Think of it like
A spreadsheet tab.

Say this

A posts table could store caption, author, video asset, and timestamp.

Rowjargon

Means
One record inside a database table.

Think of it like
One line in a spreadsheet.

Say this

One row in users equals one user.

Columnjargon

Means
One field on every row in a table.

Think of it like
A spreadsheet column like email or created_at.

Say this

A posts table may have columns for id, author_id, caption, and created_at.

Primary keyjargon

Means
A unique ID for a row.

Think of it like
A passport number for a database record.

Say this

Every user and post should have a stable ID.

Foreign keyjargon

Means
A database rule that links one table to another.

Think of it like
A receipt pointing to a real customer account.

Say this

posts.author_id should point to a valid user.

SQLjargon

Means
The language used to ask relational databases for data or changes.

Think of it like
A formal way to ask the database questions.

Say this

Knowing basic SQL helps you understand what the backend is doing.

NoSQLjargon

Means
A broad category of non-relational databases, often optimized for flexibility or extreme scale.

Think of it like
Different storage systems for different shapes of stuff, not all spreadsheets.

Say this

NoSQL can be useful later, but Postgres is the right default.

JSONBjargon

Means
A Postgres column type that stores flexible JSON data while still living inside Postgres.

Think of it like
A labeled drawer inside an organized filing cabinet for miscellaneous metadata.

Say this

JSONB is useful for flexible plan item data or provider metadata.

PostGISjargon

Means
A Postgres extension for geographic/location queries.

Think of it like
A map brain added to the database.

Say this

PostGIS matters when Ozola adds GPS, nearby trips, or location features.

pgvectorjargon

Means
A Postgres extension for storing AI embeddings and doing similarity search.

Think of it like
A way to store meaning as numbers inside the database.

Say this

Start with pgvector before adding a separate vector database.

Indexjargon

Means
A database shortcut that makes lookups faster.

Think of it like
The index at the back of a textbook.

Say this

Without indexes, the database may scan every row to find what it needs.

Joinjargon

Means
Combining data from multiple related tables in one query.

Think of it like
Looking up an order and the customer who placed it at the same time.

Say this

Feeds often join posts, users, media, and engagement data.

Transactionjargon

Means
A group of database changes that all succeed or all fail together.

Think of it like
Buying a concert ticket: payment and seat assignment must both happen or neither should.

Say this

Transactions prevent half-created posts or broken data.

Read replicajargon

Means
A copy of the database used to handle read traffic.

Think of it like
Opening extra checkout lanes that can answer questions but not change inventory.

Say this

Read replicas help when lots of users are viewing feeds.

Partitioningjargon

Means
Splitting one huge table into smaller chunks for performance and maintenance.

Think of it like
Keeping files in yearly folders instead of one giant pile.

Say this

Messages may eventually be partitioned by time.

Shardingjargon

Means
Splitting data across multiple separate databases.

Think of it like
Dividing a city phone book into separate books by neighborhood.

Say this

Sharding is powerful but complex and should be avoided until forced.

OLTPjargon

Means
Database work for the live app: many small reads and writes.

Think of it like
A cash register handling transactions all day.

Say this

Postgres is the OLTP database.

OLAPjargon

Means
Analytics work over huge historical data.

Think of it like
A business analyst studying years of receipts.

Say this

A warehouse like ClickHouse or BigQuery is OLAP.

Warehousejargon

Means
A database optimized for analytics, not live app transactions.

Think of it like
A giant archive for asking business questions.

Say this

Add a warehouse when PostHog and Postgres are not enough.

Redisjargon

Means
A very fast in-memory data store often used for caching, rate limits, and queues.

Think of it like
A sticky note on your desk instead of digging through a filing cabinet.

Say this

Redis can speed up hot feed/profile reads and back BullMQ jobs.

Cachejargon

Means
A temporary fast copy of data used to avoid repeating expensive work.

Think of it like
Keeping your most-used tools on the desk instead of in the garage.

Say this

Caching helps feeds load faster.

Cache invalidationjargon

Means
Deciding when cached data is stale and must be refreshed.

Think of it like
Throwing out old leftovers before serving them.

Say this

Caching is helpful, but invalidation bugs can show users old data.

Search enginejargon

Means
A specialized system for fast, typo-tolerant search.

Think of it like
A librarian who can find what you meant even if you misspelled it.

Say this

Start with Postgres full-text, then add Typesense or Meilisearch when needed.

Full-text searchjargon

Means
Searching natural text inside a database.

Think of it like
Finding notes that contain certain words.

Say this

Postgres full-text search is good enough early.

Typesense / Meilisearchjargon

Means
Lightweight search engines often used when database search gets too limited.

Think of it like
A dedicated search desk instead of asking the main office to do everything.

Say this

Add one when profile, plan, or content search needs typo tolerance and speed.

Vector databasejargon

Means
A database specialized for finding items that are similar in meaning.

Think of it like
A recommendation brain that groups similar vibes, not exact keywords.

Say this

Use pgvector first; graduate to Qdrant only when needed.

Embeddingjargon

Means
A list of numbers representing the meaning of text, images, videos, or behavior.

Think of it like
Turning vibes into coordinates on a map.

Say this

Embeddings can power semantic search and recommendations.

Semantic searchjargon

Means
Search based on meaning rather than exact words.

Think of it like
Searching ‘beach hike’ and finding ‘coastal trail’ even without the same words.

Say this

Semantic search can help users find similar trips or content.

Recommendation systemjargon

Means
A system that predicts what content or trips a user will like.

Think of it like
A smart friend saying, ‘You’d probably enjoy this.’

Say this

Recommendation quality depends on interaction data like views, saves, skips, and follows.

Video and media

Muxjargon

Means
A managed video platform for upload, transcoding, streaming, playback, and analytics.

Think of it like
Stripe for video infrastructure.

Say this

Mux is recommended because building video infrastructure alone is a trap.

Cloudflare Streamjargon

Means
A managed video platform with simple pricing for storage and delivery.

Think of it like
A simpler video utility bill with fewer line items.

Say this

Cloudflare Stream may be cheaper or more predictable than Mux at some scales.

Transcodingjargon

Means
Converting an uploaded video into multiple formats, sizes, and qualities.

Think of it like
Making small, medium, and large versions of the same photo/video.

Say this

Do not build transcoding yourself at launch.

Adaptive bitrate streaming / ABRjargon

Means
Video streaming that automatically changes quality based on the viewer’s connection.

Think of it like
Netflix lowering quality for a bad connection so playback does not stop.

Say this

ABR is needed for smooth video playback.

HLSjargon

Means
A common streaming format that chops video into small pieces for reliable playback.

Think of it like
Serving a movie as many small bites instead of one giant file.

Say this

HLS is part of modern adaptive video streaming.

CDNjargon

Means
A global network that serves files from locations near users.

Think of it like
Many local pizza shops instead of one faraway kitchen delivering everywhere.

Say this

Cloudflare is a CDN for web/assets; video providers use CDNs for streams.

Egressjargon

Means
Data leaving your server/cloud to users, often billed by bandwidth.

Paying for every package shipped out of your warehouse.

Video egress can become the biggest bill.

Storagejargon

Means
The cost/place for keeping files and data over time.

Think of it like
Renting warehouse shelves for your digital stuff.

Say this

Stored video minutes and media files create ongoing cost.

Object storagejargon

Means
Storage for files like images, videos, audio, and avatars.

Think of it like
A giant file bucket in the cloud.

Say this

Cloudflare R2 or Supabase Storage can store non-video media.

Cloudflare R2jargon

Means
Cloudflare’s S3-compatible object storage with no egress fees to the internet in many cases.

Think of it like
A file warehouse that does not charge the usual exit toll.

Say this

R2 is attractive for avatars, photos, and audio files.

S3jargon

Means
Amazon’s object storage service.

Think of it like
The classic cloud file bucket.

Say this

S3 is powerful, but delivery/egress and pipeline complexity matter.

CloudFrontjargon

Means
Amazon’s CDN.

Think of it like
Amazon’s network of local delivery points.

Say this

CloudFront can deliver files globally, but egress costs must be modeled.

MediaConvertjargon

Means
AWS service for converting videos into streamable formats.

Think of it like
A cloud video conversion factory.

Say this

Using MediaConvert means you operate more of the video pipeline yourself.

FFmpegjargon

Means
A powerful open-source tool for manipulating audio and video.

Think of it like
A Swiss Army knife for video files.

Say this

FFmpeg is great, but running a full video pipeline with it is not a solo-founder launch move.

Means
A provider-specific identifier used to play a video asset.

Think of it like
A ticket number for a video stream.

Say this

Keep playback IDs inside the MediaService layer so the app is not too vendor-coupled.

Video assetjargon

Means
A stored video record, including provider ID, status, duration, and playback information.

Think of it like
A library card for a video.

Say this

On-device editingjargon

Means
Editing video locally on the user’s phone instead of in the cloud.

Think of it like
Editing a photo on your phone before uploading it.

Say this

Launch with basic on-device trimming, cropping, text, filters, and music.

Cloud renderingjargon

Means
Processing/exporting videos on servers instead of phones.

Think of it like
Sending a complex project to a professional print shop.

Say this

Cloud rendering is a later-stage feature for heavy edits.
